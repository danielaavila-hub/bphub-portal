Step 2 — Read
📄 Diálogo

A: Ô moço, essas mangas estão boas? — Excuse me, are these mangos ripe?

B: As espadas estão ótimas. — The "espadas" are great.

A: OK, me dá umas 6 dessas. — OK, give me 6 of them.

B: Tá, mais alguma coisa? — OK, anything else?

A: Não, obrigada. — No, thank you.

Grammar 1
ESTAR + adjetivo — qualidade temporária
Use ESTAR (not SER) for the current condition of food:
PortuguêsEnglish
A manga está ótima.The mango is great (right now).
As frutas estão maduras.The fruits are ripe.
O café está quente.The coffee is hot.
Os abacaxis estão doces.The pineapples are sweet.

⚠️ SER is for permanent characteristics. ESTAR describes a temporary state — how something tastes or feels right now.

Grammar 2
Comprando na feira — frases essenciais
Key shopping expressions from the dialogue:
PortuguêsEnglishNote
Ô moçoExcuse me / Hey thereVocative — used to call attention to a vendor (moço = young man)
Me dá umas 6.Give me about 6.Umas = approximately — softens the number
Tá.OK. / Sure.Colloquial form of Está bem — very common in Brazil
Mais alguma coisa?Anything else?Standard phrase at any Brazilian market or shop
Não, obrigada.No, thank you.Obrigada (f.) / Obrigado (m.) — match your own gender
Phonology
🔊 Vogais nasais — ã e ão
Brazilian Portuguese nasal vowels don't exist in English. The letters ã and ão produce a nasal resonance — air flows through the nose as you speak.

Practice: manga · manhã · amanhã · tanta · estão · são · não

Tip: Try humming slightly through your nose as you say the vowel — that's the nasal quality English speakers often miss.
Culture
🌿 A feira livre
Feiras livres (open-air street markets) are a weekly institution across Brazil — every neighbourhood has one on a set day. Vendors know their produce inside out: they can tell you exactly when each fruit will be ripe and help you pick the best one. There's also an art to haggling. Brazil is one of the world's largest producers of tropical fruits: manga, abacaxi, mamão, goiaba, caju, maracujá — many of which are hard to find fresh outside the country. The espada mango mentioned in the dialogue is one of the most common varieties sold at Brazilian feiras — long, green-skinned, and very sweet when ripe.
