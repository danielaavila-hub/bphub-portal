Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Key Expressions — Greetings & Introductions

This short dialogue is packed with useful everyday expressions. Here are the key phrases and what they mean:

Portuguese English Notes
Com licença Excuse me Used to ask permission before entering a room or interrupting someone
O Professor João está aqui? Is Professor João here? Asking if someone is present — estar = to be (location)
Sou eu. That's me. / It's me. Literally "I am I" — very common Brazilian response to "Is X here?"
Em que eu posso ajudar? How can I help you? Polite, professional. Posso = I can (poder)
Meu nome é ___. My name is ___. Standard way to introduce yourself formally
Sou a aluna nova na sua sala. I'm the new student in your class. Sou + definite article — identifying yourself as a specific person
Prazer. Nice to meet you. / Pleased to meet you. Short for Muito prazer — very common in Brazil
Pode entrar. Come on in. / You may enter. Pode + infinitive = you can / you may
Senta. Take a seat. / Sit down. Informal imperative — very natural and warm in Brazilian speech
🇧🇷 Culture Note — "Com licença" vs. "Desculpa" In Brazil, com licença is used before doing something that requires permission — like entering a room, walking past someone, or interrupting a conversation. It is NOT an apology. If you step on someone's foot or make a mistake, you'd say desculpa (sorry) instead. English speakers often mix these up at first!
🔍 Grammar Spotlight — Sou eu

When the professor answers Sou eu, he's using a very Brazilian structure. The verb ser (to be) is used here to identify — "I am the one." Notice: there is no word for "That's" — Portuguese just puts the verb first: Sou eu (literally: "Am I.").

You can use this pattern to confirm your own identity too: Sou eu. — "It's me." / "That's me."

🔊 Phonology Note — "Licença" and the letter Ç The word licença contains a ç (c-cedilha). This letter always sounds like the English s — never like k. So licença is pronounced approximately "lee-SEN-sa". Brazilian Portuguese also tends to reduce unstressed vowels, so the final -a is short and light. Practice: say com li-CEN-ça three times, stressing the middle syllable.
