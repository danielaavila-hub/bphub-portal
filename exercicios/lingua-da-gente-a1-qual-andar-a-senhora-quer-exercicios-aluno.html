Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Números ordinais — Ordinal numbers

B asks for the "décimo primeiro" floor — the 11th. Ordinal numbers in Portuguese agree in gender with the noun they describe: o décimo andar (m.) but a décima aula (f.). Here's the full set you need for building floors:

# Masculino Feminino Example (andar)
1º primeiro primeira o primeiro andar — 1st floor
2º segundo segunda o segundo andar — 2nd floor
3º terceiro terceira o terceiro andar — 3rd floor
4º quarto quarta o quarto andar — 4th floor
5º quinto quinta o quinto andar — 5th floor
6º sexto sexta o sexto andar — 6th floor
7º sétimo sétima o sétimo andar — 7th floor
8º oitavo oitava o oitavo andar — 8th floor
9º nono nona o nono andar — 9th floor
10º décimo décima o décimo andar — 10th floor
11º décimo primeiro décima primeira o décimo primeiro andar — 11th floor
20º vigésimo vigésima o vigésimo andar — 20th floor
🇧🇷 Térreo vs. 1º andar In Brazil, the ground floor is called térreo (or andar térreo) — not the first floor. So the floor above ground level is o primeiro andar. This matches European numbering and differs from American English (where "first floor" = ground floor). If you take an elevator in a Brazilian building: térreo = G, primeiro andar = 1st above ground, and so on.
📖 A senhora / O senhor — Formal address

The elevator operator uses "a senhora" throughout — a respectful form of address for a woman he doesn't know. This is essential in formal service contexts in Brazil.

Form Used for Example
a senhora woman — formal / polite Qual andar a senhora quer? (What floor would you like?)
o senhor man — formal / polite O senhor precisa de ajuda? (Do you need help?)
você anyone — neutral / informal Qual andar você quer? (What floor do you want? — casual)

A senhora / o senhor take third-person singular verb forms — the same as ele/ela/você. So: "A senhora quer?" / "O senhor precisa?" — no special conjugation needed.

💡 When to use formal address In Brazil, use a senhora / o senhor in service interactions (elevators, shops, restaurants, hotels), when speaking to people older than you, or in any situation where showing respect matters. In casual conversation among friends, peers, or younger people, você is always fine. Notice that at the end of the dialogue, B says "para você também" — she replies with the informal você, which is common: the person providing a service is often more formal than the customer.
🔊 "Tá, chegamos" — elevator speech Tá = contraction of está, used here as "OK" / "Alright." Chegamos = first person plural of chegar in the preterite — literally "we arrived", meaning "we're here" or "here we are." The elevator operator uses nós chegamos (we arrived) even though he's speaking to the passenger — a polite way of including both people in the arrival, as if they traveled together.
📖 Para você também — Reciprocating good wishes

When A wishes B "bom dia", B responds with "obrigada, para você também" — thanks, same to you. This formula works for any good wish.

Someone says… You reply…
Bom dia! Bom dia! / Para você também!
Boa tarde! Boa tarde! / Igualmente!
Bom final de semana! Obrigado, para você também!
Feliz aniversário! Obrigada! (Can't reciprocate this one — it's your birthday!)

Igualmente (likewise / equally) is another common and slightly more formal way to reciprocate. Both para você também and igualmente are perfectly natural in Brazilian Portuguese.

Step 4 — Practice

1 What floor? — Quiz
Choose the correct ordinal number for each floor.
3rd floor
✅ Correct! Terceiro/terceira = 3rd. Remember: ground floor = térreo, so the 3rd floor is the third above ground level.
o décimo primeiro andar
✅ Correct! Décimo (10) + primeiro (1) = 11th. For compound ordinals above 10, you combine them just like in English.
Qual andar a senhora quer?
✅ Correct! A senhora is a formal second person — it literally means "the lady" but functions as "you" (formal, to a woman). The verb quer (3rd person singular of querer) is used with it.
Obrigada, para você também.
✅ Correct! Para você também = same to you / and you too. A very common and natural way to return a greeting or good wish.
