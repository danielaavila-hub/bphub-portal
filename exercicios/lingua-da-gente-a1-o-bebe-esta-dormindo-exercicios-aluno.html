Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Estar + gerúndio — What's happening right now

A explains the situation with "O bebê está dormindo" — the baby is sleeping (right now, at this moment). Brazilian Portuguese uses estar + verb -ndo for ongoing actions, just like English uses "is/am/are + -ing".

Subject Estar (to be) Example — gerúndio Meaning
Eu estou Eu estou dormindo. I am sleeping.
Você / Ele / Ela está O bebê está dormindo. The baby is sleeping.
A gente está A gente está falando. We are talking.
Vocês / Eles / Elas estão Eles estão cantando. They are singing.

How to form the gerúndio (-ndo):

Infinitive Remove -r Add -ndo Result
dormirdormi+ ndodormindo
falarfala+ ndofalando
comercome+ ndocomendo
cantarcanta+ ndocantando
🇧🇷 Brazil vs. Portugal This -ndo pattern is a hallmark of Brazilian Portuguese. In Portugal, people say "está a dormir" (estar + a + infinitivo). If you hear or read "está dormindo", it's Brazilian. Both mean exactly the same thing — they just sound very different!
📖 Pedir com educação — Dá licença / Fiquem quietinhos

A uses two polite request strategies in one sentence: an imperative verb (Fiquem) and a diminutive to soften the tone (quietinhos). Brazilian Portuguese is famous for this combination — it turns a command into a warm request.

Expression Literal meaning Function
Dá licença "Give permission" Polite opener — "Excuse me". Used to interrupt, ask someone to move, or get attention.
Fiquem quietos "Be quiet" (for a group) Direct imperative of ficar for vocês. Effective but blunt.
Fiquem quietinhos "Be a little quiet" The diminutive -inhos softens the command — it sounds caring, not aggressive. Same idea, much gentler tone.
🔊 Diminutivo como estratégia de polidez Adding -inho / -inha / -inhos / -inhas to adjectives, adverbs, or even verbs in commands is one of the most Brazilian ways to sound polite and warm. Um minutinho (just a tiny minute), mais baixinho (a little softer), espera só um pouquinho (just one tiny moment) — none of these mean the thing is actually small. They signal: "I'm being nice about this."
📖 Key Expressions from the Dialogue
Portuguese English Notes
Dá licença Excuse me Very common — to interrupt, get attention, or ask someone to make space. Different from com licença (said when physically passing by someone).
Por quê? Why? Por quê (two words, accented) = standalone question. Porque (one word, no accent) = because. Compare: Por quê? / Porque estou cansada.
Que que houve? What's up? / What happened? You saw this in Episode 27! Reduced from O que é que houve?. Very common informal question.
Desculpa Sorry Informal. More formal: Desculpe or Me desculpe. For big apologies: Peço desculpas. All very common in Brazil.
Eu não sabia. I didn't know. / I had no idea. Past form of saber (to know). Sabia is the imperfect — it expresses what you were unaware of. A very natural, polite way to excuse yourself.
🇧🇷 Culture Note — How Brazilians apologize In Brazilian Portuguese there are several levels of apology: Oi, desculpa (casual, light), Desculpe (slightly more formal), Me desculpe or Me perdoe (sincere), and Peço desculpas (formal, for serious situations). Adding "eu não sabia" softens any apology further — it explains that the mistake was unintentional. B uses exactly this strategy in the dialogue: Desculpa, eu não sabia.

1 What does it mean? — Expression Quiz
Each expression comes from the dialogue. Choose the correct English meaning.
Dá licença.
✅ Correct! Dá licença = "Give permission" → used as a polite "Excuse me" to get attention or interrupt. Don't confuse with Com licença (said when passing by someone physically).
O bebê está dormindo.
✅ Correct! Está dormindo = estar (is) + gerúndio (sleeping) → present progressive. The action is happening right now!
Eu não sabia.
✅ Correct! Sabia is the past (imperfect) of saber. Eu não sabia = I didn't know / I had no idea — a very natural, polite excuse.
Fiquem mais quietinhos!
✅ Correct! Fiquem = imperative of ficar for vocês. Quietinhos = quietos + -inhos diminutive. The diminutive makes the command much gentler and more polite.
