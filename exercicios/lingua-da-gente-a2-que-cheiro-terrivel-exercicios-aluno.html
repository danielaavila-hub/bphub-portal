Read the dialogue

João comes home sweaty from playing ball, and the smell doesn't go unnoticed.

A: Que cheiro terrível, que que isso?

What a terrible smell, what is that?

B: Desculpe, sou eu. Eu estava jogando bola.

Sorry, it's me. I was playing ball.

A: É você João? Bom, vai tomar banho, rapaz, vai.

It's you João? Well, go take a bath, man, go.

B: Tá, eu já vou.

OK, I'm going.

A: Vai já já, porque esse fedor está demais.

Go right now, because this stench is too much.

B: Sei, eu só queria esperar uns minutinhos antes.

I know, I just wanted to wait a few minutes first.

3
Language focus
Focus 1
Vai + infinitivo — imperativo informal
English: Go do X! (an informal spoken command)
In everyday spoken Brazilian Portuguese, vai (from ir) + infinitive gives a quick, informal command: "vai tomar banho" = "go take a bath." This is different from vamos ("let's..."), which includes the speaker — vai is a direct order to someone else.
Vai tomar banho, rapaz, vai!
Go take a bath, man, go! Vai arrumar seu quarto!
Go clean up your room!
Focus 2
Já e já já — imediatismo
English: right away, right now (already vs. doubled for urgency)
Já alone with a present-tense verb signals an immediate intention: "eu já vou" = "I'm going right now." Doubling it to já já intensifies the urgency even more: "vai já já" = "go right this instant."
Tá, eu já vou.
OK, I'm going right away. Vai já já, porque esse fedor está demais!
Go right now, because this stench is too much!
Focus 3
Vocabulário do cheiro & rapaz
Smell/sweat vocabulary + the informal vocative "rapaz"
Key vocabulary from the dialogue, plus the informal way Brazilians address a guy they know well.
Cheiro — smell
Fedor — stench
Suar — to sweat
Jogar bola — to play ball / soccer
Rapaz — man, dude (informal way to address a guy)
🚿 Culture note Brazilians tend to be quite direct with close friends and family about hygiene — telling someone to go shower after sports isn't rude, it's just practical (and often affectionate, as with A's repeated "vai, vai" here).
🔊 Phonology note In já já, both syllables are stressed almost equally and said quickly, back to back — the repetition itself carries the sense of urgency, more than any single sound change.
