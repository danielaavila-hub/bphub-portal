Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Gostar de — Saying what you like

A asks "Você gosta?" and B answers simply "Gosto." — just the verb, no pronoun. This one-word answer is completely natural in Brazilian Portuguese and sounds much more fluent than repeating the full sentence.

Subject Gostar de Short answer
Eu Eu gosto de maracujá. Gosto. (Yes, I like it.)
Você / Ele / Ela Você gosta de suco? Gosta. (Yes, she likes it.)
A gente A gente gosta de frutas. Gosta.
Vocês / Eles / Elas Eles gostam de açúcar? Gostam. (Yes, they like it.)
⚠️ Gostar ALWAYS needs "de" Unlike English "I like juice", Portuguese requires the preposition: gosto de suco. Skipping it is one of the most common beginner mistakes. The rule is simple: gostar de + noun or infinitive. Examples: Gosto de maracujá. / Gosto de cantar. / Não gosto de azedo.
📖 O que é isso? + É um pouco… — Identifying and describing

Two very practical structures appear in this dialogue: asking what something is, and describing its taste or quality with a degree word.

Structure Example Meaning
O que é isso? — O que é isso? — É suco de maracujá. What is this? — It's passion fruit juice.
É + adjective É azedo. / É doce. / É gostoso. It's sour. / It's sweet. / It's tasty.
É um pouco + adjective É um pouco azedo. It's a little sour.
É muito + adjective É muito gostoso! It's very tasty!
com / sem + noun Com açúcar. / Sem açúcar. With sugar. / Without sugar.

📖 Key Expressions from the Dialogue
Portuguese English Notes
O que é isso? What is this? One of the most useful beginner questions. Isso = this / that (neutral, nearby). Compare: O que é aquilo? = What is that (far away)?
Tu quer? Would you like some? Tu is a regional form of "you" — common in the Northeast and South of Brazil. Notice: tu quer (not tu queres as in Portugal) — Brazilians use the você verb form with tu.
Obrigado / Obrigada Thank you The form agrees with the speaker's gender, not the listener's. A woman says Obrigada; a man says Obrigado. A says Obrigada → A is a woman.
Gosto. I like it. / Yes, I do. One-word verb answer — just like answering "Do you like it?" with "I do" in English. Very natural in Brazilian Portuguese.
mas but Essential connector: Gosto, mas é azedo. = I like it, but it's sour. Use mas to add contrast or a qualifier.
🇧🇷 Culture Note — Maracujá e o Brasil Passion fruit (maracujá) is one of Brazil's most beloved fruits — intensely aromatic, tart, and tropical. You'll find it everywhere: fresh juice (suco de maracujá), mousse (mousse de maracujá), cheesecake, caipirinhas, and more. Because it can be very tart on its own, Brazilians almost always sweeten the juice with sugar or adoçante (sweetener). If you're offered suco natural in a Brazilian home, you're in for a treat — nothing compares to it fresh!
🔊 Tu vs. você — regional variation in Brazil Most Brazilians use você as the standard "you" — it works everywhere. But tu is alive and well in the Northeast (Maranhão, Pará, Fortaleza), Rio de Janeiro, and the far South (Rio Grande do Sul). The key difference from European Portuguese: Brazilians say "tu quer", not "tu queres". If you hear tu, don't panic — you can always respond with você and everyone will understand!

1 What does it mean? — Expression Quiz
Each expression comes from the dialogue. Choose the correct English meaning.
O que é isso?
✅ Correct! O que = what, é = is, isso = this/that. Together: "What is this?" — one of the most useful questions a beginner can know!
Gosto.
✅ Correct! Gosto alone = "I like it" — a natural one-word answer in Portuguese, just as fluent as the full sentence Eu gosto de maracujá.
É um pouco azedo.
✅ Correct! Um pouco = a little / a bit. Azedo = sour/tart. Compare: muito azedo = very sour.
Sem açúcar.
✅ Correct! Sem = without, com = with. Açúcar = sugar. Pair them up: com açúcar / sem açúcar — very useful for ordering food and drinks!
