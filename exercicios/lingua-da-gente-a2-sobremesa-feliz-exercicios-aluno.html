Read the dialogue

Two friends are finishing a meal and can't resist deciding on dessert.

A: E pra sobremesa, você quer pedir alguma coisa?

And for dessert, are you going to ask for anything?

B: Quero. O que é gostoso aqui?

I want to. What's tasty here?

A: Bom, realmente o mousse de maracujá é uma delícia, ah e o pudim de leite é muito bom.

Well, the passion fruit mousse is delicious, and the flan is very good.

B: Deixa eu ver, você já provou o sagu aqui?

Let me see, have you ever tried the pearl tapioca here?

A: Já. É muito bom também. E a goiabada, mmm aqui é excelente.

I have. It's really good too. And the guava paste here is excellent.

B: Não sei. Bom, para mim, que tal um quindim.

I don't know. OK, for me, how about quindim.

A: Concordo, com o quindim não dá pra errar. Eu vou pedir o creme de papaya.

I agree, you can't go wrong with quindim. I'm going to ask for the papaya cream.

B: Ah, sobremesa, pra fazer a gente feliz!

Ah, dessert, it's what makes people happy!

3
Language focus
Focus 1
Já + pretérito perfeito — perguntando sobre experiência
English: Have you ever...? (asking about life experience)
Já ("already"/"ever") paired with the pretérito perfeito asks whether someone has ever done something: "Você já provou o sagu?" = "Have you ever tried sagu?" Brazilians often answer with just one word — "Já." (Yes, I have) or "Nunca." (Never) — instead of a full sentence.
Você já provou o sagu aqui? / Já. É muito bom também.
Have you ever tried the pearl tapioca here? / I have. It's really good too. Você já foi ao Brasil? / Nunca, mas quero ir!
Have you ever been to Brazil? / Never, but I want to go!
Focus 2
Dá pra / não dá pra + infinitivo
English: it's possible to... / you can't... (casual modal expression)
Here dar doesn't mean "to give" — dá pra + infinitivo is an everyday way of saying something is (or isn't) possible. "Com o quindim não dá pra errar" = "You can't go wrong with quindim" (literally, "it's not possible to go wrong"). You'll hear "dá pra..." and "não dá pra..." constantly in casual spoken Portuguese.
Com o quindim não dá pra errar.
You can't go wrong with quindim. Dá pra você me ajudar amanhã?
Can you help me tomorrow? (is it possible for you to...?)
Focus 3
Sobremesas brasileiras & "Que tal...?"
Brazilian dessert vocabulary & making a suggestion
Some Brazilian desserts are genuinely hard to translate — the best way to learn them is to know the name and then go taste the real thing! Que tal + substantivo? is a light, friendly way to suggest something.
Mousse de maracujá — passion fruit mousse
Pudim de leite — milk flan / caramel custard
Sagu — tapioca pearls in sweet wine syrup
Goiabada — guava paste
Quindim — coconut-and-egg-yolk custard
Creme de papaya — papaya cream (with cassis liqueur, a São Paulo classic)

Que tal um quindim? — How about a quindim? (que tal + noun/infinitivo = suggestion)
🍮 Culture note Quindim, sagu, and goiabada don't have real English equivalents — they're distinctly Brazilian. Ordering "sobremesa" (dessert) after a big meal is a beloved ritual, not an afterthought, in Brazilian restaurant culture.
🔊 Phonology note Notice the nasal -im ending in quindim and pudim — pronounced through the nose, with no hard "m" or "n" sound closing the mouth, similar to a nasalized "ee." This nasal vowel pattern shows up constantly in Portuguese and takes practice for English speakers.
