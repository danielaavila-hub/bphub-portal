Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Modo Imperativo — Giving commands

In Brazilian Portuguese, the informal imperative is simply the 3rd person singular of the present indicative — the same form as você. No extra endings needed!

Infinitive Você (present) Imperative Meaning
falar você fala Fala! Speak!
levantar você levanta Levanta! Get up!
entrar você entra Entra! Come in!
comer você come Come! Eat!
sair você sai Sai! Get out! / Leave!
dormir você dorme Dorme! Sleep!

In the dialogue: "João! Levanta!" and "Sai da cama já." — both are informal commands, spoken with urgency.

📖 São X horas — Telling the time

Speaker A says "São sete horas já!" — It's already seven o'clock! Here's how to tell time in Portuguese.

Portuguese English
É uma hora. It's one o'clock.
São duas horas. It's two o'clock.
São sete horas. It's seven o'clock.
São oito e meia. It's half past eight.
São sete horas já! It's already seven o'clock!
💡 É vs. São Use é only for 1 o'clock (uma hora), because it is singular. Use são for all other times (duas, três, sete horas…) — they are plural. This is one of the most common mistakes for beginners!
📖 Key Expressions from the Dialogue
Portuguese English Notes
Levanta! Get up! Imperative of levantar. Common morning command — often repeated with growing impatience!
São sete horas já! It's already seven o'clock! Já adds urgency — it implies: this should have happened earlier. Great for expressing frustration.
Não pode ser! It can't be! / No way! Fixed idiomatic expression. B's reaction of disbelief — very natural in everyday Brazilian speech.
Sai da cama já. Get out of bed already. Imperative of sair + já = immediate action required! Já after a command signals zero patience.
Tá, já vou. OK, I'm coming. Já vou = I'm about to go / I'll be right there. A reassurance that action is imminent — even if B is still in bed!
Tá OK / Alright Short for está or está bem. One of the most frequent words in informal Brazilian speech.
🇧🇷 Culture Note — The many faces of "já" In this short dialogue, já appears three times — each with a different shade of meaning. "São sete horas já!" → already (expressing surprise and frustration). "Sai da cama já." → right now / immediately (adding urgency to a command). "Já vou." → in a moment / I'm coming (reassuring, but often said without moving). Learning to feel these nuances will make your Portuguese sound genuinely natural.
🔊 Phonology Note — "Tá, já vou" — connected speech This phrase is spoken almost as one breath in natural Brazilian speech: "tá-já-vô". The final vowel of vou tends to be reduced and the phrase flows together quickly. Listen carefully in the audio — B's voice has that sleepy, reluctant speed. Tá and né? are the two most useful words to sound natural in informal conversation.

Step 4 — Practice

1 What does it mean? — Expression Quiz
Each expression comes from the dialogue. Choose the correct English meaning.
Não pode ser!
✅ Correct! Não pode ser! is a fixed expression of disbelief or shock. Very common when something surprises you — like waking up and realising how late it is!
São sete horas já!
✅ Correct! São = it is (plural hours), já = already. The já here expresses frustration — it should have been done sooner!
Tá, já vou.
✅ Correct! Já vou = I'm about to go / I'll be right there. It's a reassurance — though in real life it often means "give me five more minutes!"
Sai da cama!
✅ Correct! Sai is the imperative of sair (to leave / get out). Da cama = from the bed. A very direct command!
