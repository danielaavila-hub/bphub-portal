Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Presente Contínuo — estar + gerúndio

The present continuous in Brazilian Portuguese is formed with estar + gerúndio (the -ndo form). It describes an action happening right now — or an action that was in progress (past continuous with estava/estavam).

Subject Estar Gerúndio Meaning
Eu estou trabalhando I am working
Você / Ele / Ela está fazendo you / he / she is doing
A gente está ouvindo we are listening
Vocês / Eles / Elas estão fazendo you (pl.) / they are doing
⏪ Past continuous — estava/estavam + gerúndio

B uses the past form: "A gente só estava…" — we were just… The past continuous describes an action that was in progress at a moment in the past.

Portuguese English
Eu estava trabalhando. I was working.
A gente estava conversando. We were talking.
Vocês estavam dormindo. You (pl.) were sleeping.
📖 Key Expressions from the Dialogue
Portuguese English Notes
Ô chefe Boss / Hey boss Vocative — used to address a superior directly. Ô is an attention-getting particle
Desculpa. Sorry. / Excuse me. Apology. Desculpa (informal) = desculpe (more formal)
Viu? You know? / You see? Conversational tag from ver (to see). Signals informality and a plea for understanding
A gente We (informal) Extremely common in Brazil — used in place of nós in everyday speech. Always takes 3rd person singular verb: a gente está
Para. Stop. Imperative of parar. Short, sharp — signals A is not interested in excuses
Não quero ouvir. I don't want to hear. Querer + infinitive — wanting/not wanting to do something
Senhora Ma'am Formal address for a woman in a position of authority. Contrast with chefe — B shifts register mid-dialogue, from informal to formal
🇧🇷 Culture Note — Chefe → Senhora: a register shift Notice that B starts with ô chefe (informal, like calling someone "boss" in English) but ends with desculpa senhora (formal). In Brazil, switching to senhora / senhor signals that you are taking the situation more seriously — it's a sign of respect when you realise you're in real trouble. This kind of register shift is very natural in Brazilian workplace interactions.
🔊 Phonology Note — "Viu?" and conversational tags Viu? (from ver, past tense) is pronounced quickly — almost like a single syllable: "viu". It functions like "you know?" or "right?" in English — seeking agreement or softening a statement. Similar Brazilian tags include né? (from não é?) and tá?. They are extremely common in informal speech and mark fluency when used naturally.

1 What does it mean? — Expression Quiz
Each expression comes from the dialogue. Choose the correct English meaning.
Viu?
✅ Correct! Viu? is a conversational tag — it softens what you're saying and seeks the listener's understanding. Very common in informal Brazilian speech.
A gente
✅ Correct! A gente = we in informal Brazilian Portuguese. It always takes a 3rd person singular verb: a gente está fazendo (not estamos).
Para.
✅ Correct! Para is the imperative of parar — to stop. Short and sharp — A is done listening to excuses!
Desculpa senhora.
✅ Correct! Senhora = ma'am. B switches from chefe (informal) to senhora (formal) — a respectful register shift when things get serious.
Não quero ouvir.
✅ Correct! Querer + infinitive = to want to do something. Não quero ouvir — I don't want to hear (it).
