Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Fugir — to run away / to escape

A says "O Bibo fugiu mesmo." — fugir is an irregular -ir verb. The key forms to know at this level are the present and the preterite.

Pronoun Presente Pretérito perfeito
eu fujo fugi
você / ele / ela foge fugiu
nós fugimos fugimos
eles / vocês fogem fugiram

The word mesmo after the verb adds emphasis — it's like "really" or "actually": "Fugiu mesmo." = He really did run away. / He actually ran away. You can also hear mesmo used for "even" (mesmo assim = even so / still) and "same" (o mesmo = the same one).

💡 Mesmo — three jobs in one word Mesmo is one of those ultra-versatile Brazilian words: (1) emphasis — Fugiu mesmo. (He really ran away.); (2) even so / still — Mesmo assim, vai ficar chateado. (Even so, he's going to be upset.); (3) same — O mesmo problema. (The same problem.) Context always makes the meaning clear.
📖 O imperfeito — Habitual past actions

B uses two imperfect forms: "ninguém brincava mais" and "só dava trabalho." The pretérito imperfeito describes what used to happen — ongoing or repeated actions in the past, in contrast to the preterite which describes completed events.

Verb Imperfeito (ele/ela) Meaning here
brincar (to play) brincava used to play / would play (habitually)
dar (to give / to cause) dava used to cause / kept causing (habitually)

Só dava trabalho — literally "only gave work" — is a set phrase meaning "was always causing trouble / was nothing but work." Dar trabalho = to be a burden / to cause a lot of effort.

🔊 Ninguém… mais — negative concord B says "ninguém brincava mais com ele" — literally "nobody played more with him" = nobody played with him anymore. In Portuguese, two (or more) negatives strengthen each other rather than cancelling out — unlike in English. This is called concordância negativa. You already saw it in Episode 33: "já não tem mais nada" (there's nothing left). Both ninguém (nobody) and mais (anymore) are negative in this context, and together they make the negation stronger, not positive.
📖 Vai ficar + adjective — Talking about future emotional states

A predicts: "Marquinhos vai ficar chateado." — vai ficar (is going to be / is going to feel) is used with adjectives to describe an emotional or physical state in the near future. Ficar + adjective is extremely common in Brazilian Portuguese for expressing how someone feels or becomes.

Portuguese English Notes
chateado/a upset / annoyed / bothered Very common — milder than com raiva (angry)
aliviado/a relieved From alívio (relief)
feliz happy Invariable — same for masc. and fem.
triste sad Invariable — same for masc. and fem.
surpreso/a surprised Agrees in gender: surpreso (m.) / surpresa (f.)
preocupado/a worried From preocupar (to worry)

Ficar + adjective describes a change of state or emotional reaction — like English "to get" or "to feel": Ela ficou chateada. (She got upset.) Vai ficar aliviado. (He's going to be relieved.)

Step 4 — Practice

1 What does it mean? — Expression Quiz
Choose the correct meaning for each expression from the dialogue.
O Bibo fugiu mesmo.
✅ Correct! Fugiu is the preterite of fugir (he/she/it ran away). Mesmo here = really / indeed. Together: Bibo actually ran away — it really happened.
Não culpo ele.
✅ Correct! Culpar = to blame. Culpo = I blame (1st person present). Ele used as direct object (informal) = him. So: I don't blame him.
Ninguém brincava mais com ele.
✅ Correct! Ninguém = nobody. Brincava = imperfect of brincar (used to play). Mais with negation = anymore. Together: nobody played with him anymore (in the past, habitually).
O Bibo só dava trabalho.
✅ Correct! Só = only / just. Dava = imperfect of dar (used to give). Dar trabalho = to be a burden / to cause trouble. So: Bibo was nothing but trouble.
