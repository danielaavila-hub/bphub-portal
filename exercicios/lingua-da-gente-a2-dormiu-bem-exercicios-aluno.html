Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Que horas são? — Telling the Time

A replies "Seis e meia" — six-thirty. Here's how to ask and tell the time in Brazilian Portuguese.

Clock Portuguese Note
1:00 É uma hora. Singular — only 1:00 uses é
6:00 São seis horas. Plural — use são for all other times
6:15 São seis e quinze. six and fifteen
6:30 São seis e meia. meia = half → from the dialogue!
6:45 São seis e quarenta e cinco. or: sete menos quinze
12:00 É meio-dia. noon — singular!
00:00 É meia-noite. midnight — singular!
💡 É vs. São — the most common mistake Use é only for 1 o'clock, noon, and midnight (all singular). Use são for everything else (plural). "São seis e meia" — not "é seis e meia".
📖 Vamos + infinitivo — Making Suggestions

B says "Vamos dormir um pouco mais!" — this is the most natural way to say "let's…" in Brazilian Portuguese.

Infinitive Suggestion English
dormir Vamos dormir um pouco mais! Let's sleep a little more!
comer Vamos comer? Shall we eat?
trabalhar Vamos trabalhar! Let's get to work!
sair Vamos sair? Shall we go out?
começar Vamos começar! Let's start!

💡 Vamos with a ? sounds like an invitation; with a ! it's more enthusiastic. Both are very common in everyday Brazilian speech.

📖 Key Expressions from the Dialogue
Portuguese English Notes
Dormiu bem? Did you sleep well? From dormir (to sleep). Pretérito perfeito: você dormiu.
Mais ou menos. More or less. / So-so. Very common in Brazil — a relaxed, honest answer. Brazilians avoid extremes in casual conversation.
Que horas são? What time is it? Literally: "What hours are they?" Always plural são in the question.
Seis e meia. Six-thirty. Meia = half. So "six and a half" = 6:30. Also used for "meia" = 6 in phone numbers!
Ainda é muito cedo. It's still really early. Ainda = still. Cedo = early (opposite: tarde = late).
Um pouco / pouquinho. A little / a tiny bit. Pouquinho is the diminutive — smaller and warmer than pouco. Very Brazilian!
Tá. OK / Alright. Informal short form of está bem. One of the most frequent words in spoken Brazilian Portuguese.
🇧🇷 Culture Note — Diminutives & "mais ou menos" Two things will make your Portuguese sound instantly more Brazilian. First: use pouquinho instead of pouco — diminutives signal warmth and are everywhere in Brazilian speech (cafezinho, beijinho, rapidinho). Second: reply mais ou menos when someone asks how you are — it's honest, relaxed, and very natural. A simple bem can sound formal or even cold to Brazilian ears.
🔊 Phonology Note — Nasal vowels Brazilian Portuguese has nasal vowels that don't exist in English. In this dialogue, notice bom and ainda: the vowel resonates through the nose, not just the mouth. The tilde (~) always marks a nasal vowel: não, irmã, então. Vowels before m or n at the end of a syllable are also nasalized: bom, um, com. Try saying bom dia — let your voice go slightly nasal on the o, as if humming. Listen to the episode and focus on how bom sounds different from the English word "bomb."

Step 4 — Practice

1 What does it mean? — Expression Quiz
Each expression comes from the dialogue. Choose the correct English meaning.
Mais ou menos.
✅ Correct! Mais ou menos is one of the most useful expressions in Brazilian Portuguese — a relaxed way to say "so-so" or "not too bad." You'll hear it constantly.
Vamos dormir um pouco mais!
✅ Correct! Vamos + infinitive = "let's…". Here: vamos dormir = let's sleep. Um pouco mais = a little more.
Ainda é muito cedo.
✅ Correct! Ainda = still. Cedo = early. Muito = very/really. The opposite: ainda é muito tarde = it's still really late.
Tá, mais um pouquinho só.
✅ Correct! Tá = OK. Pouquinho = tiny bit (diminutive of pouco). Só adds the idea of "just/only." A very natural, soft agreement.
