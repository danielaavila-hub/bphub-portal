B: É, você vai para a festinha dela?

English:
A: Tomorrow is Bete's birthday, right?
B: Yeah, are you going to her little party?
A: Do I need to bring anything?
B: No, it's really an informal thing.
Step 3 — Language notes
📖 Grammar & Vocabulary
Grammar 1 — IR + para / IR + infinitivo (near future)

The verb ir (to go) is used in two important ways at this level:

UseExampleMeaning
IR + para (place)Você vai para a festa?Are you going to the party?
IR + infinitivo (future)Vai chover amanhã.It's going to rain tomorrow.

Full conjugation of IR (present):

PersonFormExample
euvouEu vou à festa.
você / ele / elavaiEla vai amanhã.
a gente / nósvai / vamosA gente vai junto.
vocês / eles / elasvãoEles vão de carro.
Grammar 2 — Não é? (tag question) & mesmo (intensifier)

Não é? is the most common tag question in Brazilian Portuguese — equivalent to "right?", "isn't it?", "don't you think?" It stays the same regardless of the subject:

StatementTag
Amanhã é o aniversário da Bete,não é?
Você é americano,não é?
Está frio hoje,não é?

Mesmo after an adjective adds emphasis: é informal mesmo = "it's really informal / it's informal, seriously."

🔊 Phonology — Diminutivo: -inha / -inho

Brazilians frequently add -inha / -inho to nouns to express smallness, affection, or informality:

  • festa → festinha — a little party / a casual get-together
  • café → cafezinho — a coffee (common social ritual)
  • momento → momentinho — just a moment

Notice the stress: fes-TI-nha. The -inha/-inho suffix is always stressed.

🇧🇷 Culture — Festas brasileiras

Brazilian parties are famously informal and flexible. There is typically no fixed start or end time — guests arrive when they can and stay as long as they like. Saying é uma coisa bem informal mesmo signals that no special preparation is needed: dress casually, come empty-handed, just show up. This relaxed attitude is central to Brazilian social culture.
