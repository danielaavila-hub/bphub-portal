Read the dialogue

Two friends talking. One is studying Portuguese and venting about how hard it is.

A: Você está estudando o português?

B: Estou, mas a pronúncia é muito difícil, viu?

A: Como assim?

B: Esse tal de 'avô – avó', 'pode – pôde', eu não ouço a diferença.

A: Sei, inicialmente causa problemas, mas você vai pegar, não se preocupe.

B: 'Não se preocupe' ou 'Não se preocupa'?

A: Tanto faz, qualquer um dos dois.

B: Te disse, não é? O português não é fácil não.

A: Are you studying Portuguese?

B: I am, but the pronunciation is really hard, you know?

A: What do you mean?

B: This business with 'avô – avó', 'pode – pôde' — I can't hear the difference.

A: I know, at first it causes problems, but you'll get it — don't worry.

B: 'Não se preocupe' or 'Não se preocupa'?

A: It doesn't matter, either one works.

B: I told you, didn't I? Portuguese is not easy.

Language focus
Focus 1
Estar + -ndo — the BP progressive
English: I am studying / she is working…
Brazilian Portuguese strongly prefers the gerund construction estar + verb-ndo for actions in progress — much more than European Portuguese (which uses estar a + infinitivo). Notice B's short answer: Estou (not Sim) — in BP, you echo the verb of the question for a more natural confirmation.

Focus 2
Avô vs Avó — vogais abertas e fechadas
Open vs closed vowels: the accent mark distinction
This is one of the trickiest features of BP phonology. The circumflex (^) marks a closed vowel and the acute (´) marks an open vowel. But the words in each pair have completely different meanings — and in standard BP, the circumflex ô and the acute ó sound noticeably different.

Avô (grandfather) has a tighter, more closed /o/. Avó (grandmother) has a wider, more open /ɔ/. Same with pôde (past: he was able) vs pode (present: he can).
ô
Circunflexo — vogal fechada
avô / pôde / avô
grandfather / he was able to
ó
Agudo — vogal aberta
avó / pode / só
grandmother / he can / only
Meu avô tem 80 anos. Minha avó tem 78.
My grandfather is 80. My grandmother is 78. Ela pode vir hoje? — Ela pôde vir ontem.
Can she come today? — She was able to come yesterday.
Focus 3
Expressões do diálogo
Key dialogue expressions
This episode is packed with authentic, high-frequency expressions. Notice that many of them are reassuring or conversational — ideal for any learner's toolkit.
Como assim? — What do you mean? / How so?
Used when something is unclear or surprising. Esse tal de… — This business/deal with… / This thing called…
Informal way to refer to something puzzling. Você vai pegar — You'll get it / you'll pick it up
Reassurance: pegar = to get/understand informally. Não se preocupe / Não se preocupa — Don't worry
Both are correct in BP — imperative vs indicative used as request. Tanto faz — It doesn't matter / either way / whatever
Relaxed acceptance — very common in BP. Qualquer um dos dois — Either one (of the two)
Te disse — I told you (so)
Colloquial BP — te = indirect object before the verb.
🌎 Culture note Tanto faz encapsulates something important about Brazilian communication style: a relaxed flexibility and willingness to adapt. Where some cultures insist on precise rules, BP speakers often say tanto faz — acknowledging that language is alive and multiple forms can coexist. The tolerance for variation (não se preocupe / não se preocupa) is real and reflects how BP has evolved.
🔊 Phonology note The open/closed vowel contrast (avô / avó) is easier to hear than to produce at first. In BP, the open ó sounds like the vowel in English "off" or "law", while the closed ô is more like the vowel in "go" but without the glide. Tip: exaggerate your mouth opening for ó and keep lips more rounded for ô. Your ear will train over time!
