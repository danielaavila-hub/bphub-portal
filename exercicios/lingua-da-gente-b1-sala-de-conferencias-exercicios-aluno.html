Foco Gramatical

Ir + Infinitivo — Expressing Future Plans

The most common way to talk about future plans and intentions in Brazilian Portuguese is ir (conjugated) + infinitivo. It's the equivalent of English going to + verb. This dialogue is packed with examples — notice how the object pronoun can attach directly to the infinitive with a hyphen (usá-la).

SubjectIr (present)+ InfinitivoExample from the dialogue
euvou+ verb"Não sei, mas vou verificar."
você / ele / elavai+ verb"Carlinhos vai usá-la amanhã de manhã."
nósvamos+ verb"Queremos revisar o orçamento." (→ vamos revisar)
eles / elasvão+ verb"Eles vão usá-la até o almoço."

Note: Usá-la = usar + a (ela) — in written and formal speech, the direct object pronoun attaches to the infinitive. In casual speech, Brazilians often say simply "vão usar ela". Both are correct in different registers.

Negation: Put não before ir: "Hoje não vai dar mesmo" — Today it's just not going to work out.

Foco Pragmático

"A senhora" e "O senhor" — Formal Address in Brazilian Workplaces

In this dialogue, Antônio consistently calls his boss a senhora — not você. This is the formal register used in many Brazilian professional settings, particularly when speaking to someone of higher status. It's the equivalent of addressing someone as ma'am or sir in English.

ContextHow to addressExample
Casual / peersvocê"Você sabe o horário da reunião?"
Formal / senior colleaguea senhora / o senhor"A senhora quer que eu faça a reserva?"
Customer service (shops, hotels)a senhora / o senhor"O senhor gostaria de ver o cardápio?"
Elderly person (any context)a senhora / o senhor"Posso ajudar a senhora?"

Key rule: When using a senhora / o senhor, the verb takes the 3rd person singular form — same as ele/ela: "A senhora quer…?", "O senhor prefere…?"

Tip: Not sure which to use? In professional and service contexts, starting with a senhora / o senhor and waiting for the other person to invite a more casual register ("pode me chamar de você") is always the safer choice.
