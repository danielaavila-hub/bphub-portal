Read the dialogue

Andreia can't stop staring at guys working out at the gym. Cassia is not impressed — by the guys, or by Andreia's excuse.

A: Uau, olha aquele cara que está fazendo abdominais.

Wow, look at that guy who is doing sit-ups.

B: Para, Andréia, você sempre está olhando os homens. É uma doença isso, viu?

Stop it, Andreia, you are always looking at men. It's a sickness, you know?

A: Não Cássia, é que eu aprecio o corpo de quem malha, saradão.

No Cassia, I just appreciate the bodies of those who work out, so ripped.

B: "Aprecio o corpo de quem malha" — não vem com essa não.

"You appreciate the bodies of those who work out" — please don't give me that.

A: Não, olha pra aquele na barra, aquele ali, você não acha legal ver alguém com esses músculos?

No, look at that guy at the barbells, that one there, don't you think it's cool to see someone with those muscles?

B: Não, eu só penso em esteroides e suplementos artificiais.

No, I just think of steroids and artificial supplements.

A: Bom, eu acho lindo.

Well, I think it's beautiful.

B: E eu acho que não é nada natural.

And I think it is not natural at all.

Language focus
Focus 1
Quem — "the one(s) who" / "whoever"
English: relative pronoun without a named antecedent
You already know quem as a question word ("who?"). But quem also works as a relative pronoun meaning "the person/people who" or "whoever," with no name attached: "eu aprecio o corpo de quem malha" = "I appreciate the body of whoever works out / of people who work out." It replaces a whole idea ("as pessoas que malham") with one small word.

Com nome (que)

o corpo das pessoas que malham

o cara que está na barra

Sem nome (quem)

o corpo de quem malha

Quem quiser, pode vir

Eu aprecio o corpo de quem malha.
I appreciate the body of whoever works out. Quem chegar primeiro, ganha.
Whoever arrives first, wins. (a common everyday pattern with quem)
Focus 2
Achar + adjetivo vs. Achar que + oração
English: to find/think (something is) X vs. to think that...
Achar means both "to find" and "to think" in the sense of opinion. When followed directly by an adjective, it means "to find/consider something [adjective]": "Eu acho lindo" (I find it beautiful). When followed by que + a full clause, it introduces a fuller opinion: "Eu acho que não é nada natural" (I think that it's not natural at all). Both are everyday ways to give an opinion — choose based on whether you need a short reaction or a full sentence.
acho lindoachar + adjective
acho legalachar + adjective
acho que não é naturalachar que + clause
Você não acha legal ver alguém com esses músculos?
Don't you find it cool to see someone with those muscles? Eu acho que não é nada natural.
I think that it's not natural at all.
Focus 3
Vocabulário da academia & expressões do diálogo
Gym vocabulary & everyday expressions
Key gym/fitness vocabulary and colloquial reactions from this teasing exchange between friends.
Malhar — to work out
Abdominais — sit-ups / abs
A barra — the barbell / pull-up bar
Músculos — muscles
Saradão — super ripped/jacked (informal, augmentative slang)
Esteroides / suplementos — steroids / supplements

Para! — Stop it! (bare informal imperative, no "de + infinitivo" needed)
É uma doença isso — "It's a sickness/obsession" (playful exaggeration)
Não vem com essa não — "Don't give me that" (colloquial double negative for emphasis)
🌎 Culture note Academia (gym) culture is huge in Brazil, and slang for physique is colorful and specific: saradão/saradona describes someone visibly very fit, often used admiringly among friends — exactly as Andreia uses it here. Teasing a friend about "always looking" is also a very common, affectionate way friends talk to each other in Portuguese.
📝 Word-formation note Notice the augmentative suffix -ão in saradão (from "sarado" + "-ão"): it intensifies the meaning, similar to adding "super-" or "really" in English. You'll see this pattern often in informal speech: chato → chatão, grande → grandão.
