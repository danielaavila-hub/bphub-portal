Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Ter que + infinitivo — Obligation

B says "você tem que usar essa pulseira o dia todo" — ter que + infinitive is the most common way to express obligation in Brazilian Portuguese, equivalent to "have to / must / need to."

Pronoun Ter que + infinitivo English
eu tenho que trabalhar I have to work
você / ele / ela tem que usar a pulseira you/he/she has to wear the bracelet
nós temos que sair cedo we have to leave early
eles / vocês têm que estudar they/you all have to study
💡 Ter que vs. Ter de vs. Precisar All three express obligation, but with slightly different registers. Ter que is the most common in everyday speech — natural and casual. Ter de is more formal or written. Precisar de (to need to) is slightly softer — more of a necessity than a strict obligation. At this level, ter que is the one to master: Tenho que ir. (I have to go.) Você tem que ver esse filme! (You have to see this film!)
📖 Quantos passos você deu? — Dar + passos

B asks "quantos passos você já deu hoje?" — In Portuguese, steps are "given" (dar passos), not "taken" as in English. Deu is the preterite of dar (3rd person singular / você). You already saw deu in Episode 39 (the medicine Marina gave me).

Question word Use Example
Quantos (m. pl.) How many — with masculine plural nouns Quantos passos você deu? (How many steps did you take?)
Quantas (f. pl.) How many — with feminine plural nouns Quantas horas você dormiu? (How many hours did you sleep?)
Quanto (m. sg.) How much Quanto custa? (How much does it cost?)

Other common expressions with dar: dar uma olhada (to take a look), dar um passeio (to take a walk / stroll), dar certo (to work out / go well), dar trabalho (to be a burden — Episode 37!).

📖 Expressões-chave — Não ligo, nada mal, o dia todo
Expression Meaning Notes
Eu não ligo. I don't mind. / I don't care. Ligar para = to care about. Without the para in speech: não ligo = I don't mind / it doesn't bother me. Casual and very common.
Nada mal. Not bad. Fixed phrase — used as a mild compliment or to express mild satisfaction. Nada = nothing; mal = bad. Literally "nothing bad." Equivalent to "not bad at all."
O dia todo. All day / the whole day. Todo/toda + article + time noun = the whole ___. A semana toda (all week), o ano todo (all year), a noite toda (all night).
Deixa eu ver. Let me see. Very natural filler — used when checking something, pausing to think, or looking something up. Deixa eu pensar = let me think. Deixa eu verificar = let me check.
Sabe que… You know, … / Did you know that… Used to introduce a piece of information — like English "you know what?" or "get this." Very common conversation opener in Brazilian Portuguese.
🔊 Numbers with periods and commas In Brazil (and most of continental Europe), the decimal separator is a comma and the thousands separator is a period — the opposite of English. So 9,850 steps in the dialogue is written 9.850 in Portuguese. And R$ 1.500,00 = R$ 1,500.00. This trips up many English speakers when reading prices or statistics in Brazil!

Step 4 — Practice

1 Ter que — Obligation Quiz
Choose the correct form of ter que for each sentence.
Eu ___ usar o Fitbit o dia todo.
✅ Correct! Tenho que = I have to (1st person singular of ter). Tenho que usar o Fitbit o dia todo. = I have to wear the Fitbit all day.
Você ___ dar pelo menos 10.000 passos por dia.
✅ Correct! Tem que = you/he/she has to (3rd person singular). Você tem que dar 10.000 passos. = You have to take 10,000 steps.
Eu não ligo.
✅ Correct! Ligar para = to care about / to mind. Não ligo (without para) = I don't mind / I don't care. It sounds like "I don't call" but in this context it means "it doesn't bother me."
Nada mal.
✅ Correct! Nada mal = not bad / not bad at all. A handy, casual expression of mild approval or pleasant surprise — "9,850 steps, not bad!"
