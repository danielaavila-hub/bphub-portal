Grammar 1
ESTAR + gerúndio — o presente contínuo
To say what is happening right now, use ESTAR + verb ending in -ndo:
InfinitivoGerúndioExample
prepararpreparandoEstou preparando o jantar.
lavarlavandoVou lavando as mãos.
fazerfazendoEstou fazendo moqueca.
comercomendoEstamos comendo agora.
servirservindoEla está servindo o prato.

Formation: drop the infinitive ending (-ar/-er/-ir) → add -ando (for -ar verbs) or -endo/-indo (for -er/-ir verbs).

Grammar 2
"Não é?" — a tag question · "Vai + infinitivo" — o imperativo suave
Two communicative shortcuts from the dialogue:
ExpressãoMeaningNotes
Moqueca, não é?Moqueca, right?"Não é?" = the all-purpose tag question in BP. Used to confirm a guess or seek agreement.
Vai lavar as mãos.Go wash your hands."Vai + infinitive" is a soft imperative — warmer than a formal command. Very common in family/home settings.

More "vai + infinitivo" examples: Vai dormir. (Go to sleep.) · Vai comer. (Go eat.) · Vai buscar. (Go get it.)

Phonology
🔊 O ditongo "ei" — cheiro, gostoso, jantar
The diphthong "ei" in Brazilian Portuguese is a falling diphthong — the stress lands on the first vowel, then glides down. It sounds like "ay" in "say," but shorter and more closed.

Practice: cheiro · feio · sei · lei · primeiro · leite · beijo

Also notice the ch sound in "cheiro" — in Brazilian Portuguese, ch = "sh" (as in "shoe"), not "ch" as in "cheese."

Contrast: cheiro (sh-AY-roo) · chave (SHA-vee) · chuva (SHOO-vah)
Culture
🍲 Moqueca — o orgulho da culinária brasileira
Moqueca is a rich Brazilian fish stew and one of the country's most iconic dishes. There are two great regional versions: moqueca baiana (from Bahia), made with coconut milk, dendê oil, tomatoes, onion, garlic, coriander, and fish — golden and fragrant; and moqueca capixaba (from Espírito Santo), lighter, made without coconut milk, slow-cooked in a traditional clay pot called panela de barro. Brazilians from both states defend their version passionately. The dish goes back centuries, rooted in Indigenous and African culinary traditions. The word moqueca likely comes from Tupi moquém (a way of cooking fish over fire).
