Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Para mim, para você, para ele — Pronouns after prepositions

A distributes things "para você… para ele" — and B asks "E pra mim?". In Portuguese, the pronoun that follows a preposition is slightly different from the subject pronoun. The most important change: eu → mim.

Subject pronoun After preposition (para, de, com…) Example
eu (I) mim ⚠️ Essa é para mim. This is for me.
você (you) você ✓ Essa é para você. This is for you.
ele / ela (he / she) ele / ela ✓ Essa é para ele. This is for him.
a gente / nós (we) a gente / nós ✓ Isso é para a gente? Is this for us?
vocês / eles / elas (you pl. / they) vocês / eles / elas ✓ Esse presente é para eles. This gift is for them.
💡 Only "eu" changes — everything else stays the same The key insight: eu → mim is the only irregular form after prepositions. Never say "para eu" — always "para mim". All other pronouns stay unchanged. Special case: with com (with), mim becomes comigo: Você vai comigo? = Are you coming with me? (Not "com mim".)
🔊 "Para" → "pra" in spoken Brazilian In everyday speech, Brazilians almost always shorten para to pra (or even pro / pra before articles): pra mim, pra você, pro João, pra escola. You'll rarely hear the full para in casual conversation — it sounds formal or slow. When writing, use para; when speaking, pra is completely natural.
📖 Acabou + Já não tem mais nada — It's all gone!

A delivers bad news: "acabou, já não tem mais nada." — it's all gone, there's nothing left. These are two of the most useful and everyday expressions in Brazilian Portuguese.

Expression Meaning More examples
Acabou. It's all gone. / It's over. / It ran out. Acabou o café. (The coffee ran out.)
Acabou a festa. (The party's over.)
Não tem mais. There's no more. / There isn't any more. Não tem mais suco. (There's no more juice.)
Já não tem mais nada. There's nothing left anymore. Stacks three negatives: já (already/anymore) + não (not) + mais (more) + nada (nothing). In Portuguese, multiple negatives reinforce — they don't cancel out like in English.
💡 Negação múltipla em português "Já não tem mais nada" has four negative elements in a row — and it's grammatically perfect. Portuguese (unlike English) uses negative concord: each negative word reinforces the others. Não tem nada, não vejo ninguém, nunca fui a lugar nenhum — all correct, all emphatic. The more negatives, the stronger the denial!
📖 Key Expressions — Disappointment and Reactions
Portuguese English Notes
E pra mim? And for me? Short question with big emotional weight — B's single line says everything. Pra = spoken form of para.
Ah desculpa. Oh, I'm sorry. Ah = interjection of mild realisation or regret. Desculpa = informal apology. Together: a gentle, slightly embarrassed sorry.
Droga! Bummer! / Darn! / Shoot! A mild expletive — completely safe to use in all situations. Droga literally means "drug" but as an interjection it's just an expression of frustration, similar to English "Darn!" or "Shoot!"
E agora? Now what? / What now? A very Brazilian expression of "what do we do from here?" — used when a plan falls apart or something unexpected happens. Agora = now.
🇧🇷 Culture Note — Brazilian expressions of frustration Brazilians have a rich vocabulary for mild frustration that manages to sound warm even when disappointed. Droga! is the go-to mild expletive — you'll hear it constantly. Other common ones: Puxa! (Wow! / Darn! — surprise or mild regret), Que pena! (What a shame! / Too bad!), Nossa! (Wow! — surprise in general), Ai ai… (Oh well… — resigned). All are safe in any company. The Brazilians' ability to express frustration with warmth and humor is itself a cultural characteristic.

1 What does it mean? — Expression Quiz
Each expression comes from the dialogue. Choose the correct English meaning.
Acabou.
✅ Correct! Acabar = to finish / to run out. Acabou (past tense) = it ran out / it's all gone / it's over — one of the most useful everyday Brazilian expressions.
E pra mim?
✅ Correct! Pra = spoken form of para (for). Mim = me (pronoun after prepositions — eu changes to mim after para, de, com…).
Droga!
✅ Correct! Droga! is a mild, safe interjection of frustration — equivalent to "Darn!", "Shoot!", or "Bummer!" in English. Completely acceptable in any company.
E agora?
✅ Correct! Agora = now. E agora? = "Now what?" / "What do we do now?" — a very Brazilian way of expressing "what's the next step?" after something goes wrong.
