Step 2 — Read the dialogue
💬 Diálogo
Read along while you listen. Notice how Vai chover expresses a future prediction and how fazer o quê? accepts an unavoidable situation.

A: Ai, que calor, não aguento mais!

B: Realmente, está insuportável.

A: Vai chover essa semana?

B: Nada, mas, fazer o quê?

English:
A: Oh, it's so hot, I can't take it anymore!
B: Really, this is unbearable.
A: Is it going to rain this week?
B: Not at all, but what can you do?
Step 3 — Language notes
📖 Grammar & Vocabulary
Grammar 1 — ESTAR + adjetivo: describing the weather

Use estar (not ser) for weather states — they are temporary and changeable:

PortugueseEnglish
Está quente.It's hot.
Está frio.It's cold.
Está insuportável.It's unbearable.
Está nublado.It's cloudy.
Está úmido.It's humid.

For exclamations, use Que + noun: Que calor! (What heat! / It's so hot!), Que frio! (It's freezing!)

Grammar 2 — VAI + infinitivo: near future (prediction)

To talk about what is going to happen, use vai (or other forms of ir) + infinitive:

PortugueseEnglish
Vai chover essa semana.It's going to rain this week.
Vai fazer calor amanhã.It's going to be hot tomorrow.
Vai esfriar no fim de semana.It's going to get cooler this weekend.

Fazer o quê? is a fixed expression meaning "what can you do?" / "nothing to be done about it" — used to accept something unavoidable with resignation.

🔊 Phonology — Exclamações: Ai! / Que + noun

Ai! is a universal Brazilian exclamation of pain, surprise, or exasperation — it functions like "Oh!", "Ow!", or "Ugh!" depending on context. The intonation rises and falls quickly.

Note the vowels in key words:

  • calor — stress on the last syllable: ca-LOR
  • insuportável — stress on -tá-: in-su-por-TÁ-vel
  • chover — the ch sounds like English "sh": sho-ver
🇧🇷 Culture — O tempo no Brasil

Brazil has diverse climates, but heat and rain dominate in many regions. In cities like São Paulo and Rio de Janeiro, complaining about the heat (reclamar do calor) is a national pastime. The rainy season (estação chuvosa) runs roughly from November to March in southeastern Brazil, bringing relief from heat but also flooding. Fazer o quê? — the resigned acceptance of weather and other uncontrollable things — reflects a broader Brazilian attitude of flexibility in the face of unpredictability.
