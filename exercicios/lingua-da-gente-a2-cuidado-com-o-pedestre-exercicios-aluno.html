Read the dialogue

A nervous passenger keeps warning the driver about cars, pedestrians, and motorcycles.

A: Olha o carro!

Watch out for the car!

B: Tá, já vi.

OK, I saw it.

A: Cuidado com o pedestre!

Careful with the pedestrian!

B: Tá, já sei!

OK, I know!

A: Olha lá, tá freando, ô!

Watch out, he's braking there!

B: Para Cecilia, você quer dirigir?

Stop it Cecilia, do you want to drive?

A: Não, tudo bem..., cuidado, cuidado!

No, it's fine…, careful, careful!

B: Que que foi?

What was it?

A: Ah desculpa, pensei que o motoqueiro ia cortar em frente.

Oh, sorry, I thought that motorcycle driver was going to cut in front.

B: Sinceramente Cecilia, calma aí menina!

Seriously Cecilia, calm down girl!

3
Language focus
Focus 1
Olha! / Cuidado! — Warning imperatives
English: look out! / careful! (traffic warnings)
Olha! and Cuidado com...! are the go-to expressions for warning someone about danger — extremely common while riding in a car or walking together.
Olha o carro!
Watch out for the car!Cuidado com o pedestre!
Careful with the pedestrian!
Focus 2
Pensei que + imperfeito do subjuntivo/ia + infinitivo — Reporting a mistaken thought
English: I thought that (something was going to happen)
Pensei que + ia + infinitive reports a past belief or expectation that turned out to be wrong — a natural way to explain a false alarm.
Pensei que o motoqueiro ia cortar em frente.
I thought the motorcycle was going to cut in front.Pensei que ia chover.
I thought it was going to rain.
Focus 3
Que que foi? — What happened? (casual)
English: what was that? / what happened?
Que que foi? (casual contraction of o que é que foi) is used to ask what just happened, especially after a sudden reaction.
Que que foi?
What happened?Que que foi isso?
What was that?
🚗 Passageiro do banco de trás The "backseat driver" phenomenon is universal! In Brazilian Portuguese, being overly cautious as a passenger is sometimes teasingly called being manteiga derretida (melted butter) — overly nervous or worried.
🔊 Calma aí, menina! Calma aí is a very common, gentle way to tell someone to relax — softer than a direct command, often used affectionately between friends.
