O Futebol É Muito Violento

Read the dialogue

Two friends are watching a soccer match. After two players collide going for the ball, they end up disagreeing about whether the sport is too violent.

A: Olha lá!

Look at that!

B: Que que houve rapaz?

What happened, man?

A: Os dois bateram a cabeça quando iam para a bola.

The two hit their heads when they were going for the ball.

B: Realmente o futebol é muito violento. Não sei por que vocês assistem esses jogos, viu?

Soccer really is very violent. I don't know why you watch these games.

A: Bom, perigoso pode ser, mas é muito lindo também. E realmente é impressionante ver a capacidade deles.

Well, it can be dangerous, but it's beautiful too. And it's really impressive to see their ability.

B: Bom, eu só vejo gente machucada, lesionada, e vocês assistindo como se fosse diversão.

Well, I just see injured, hurt people, and you watching as if it were entertainment.

A: Ah, não fale assim, você sabe que quem joga se identifica com a ação no campo.

Oh, don't talk like that, you know that those who play identify with the action on the field.

B: Bom, ainda acho um absurdo elogiar tanta violência, viu?

Well, I still think it's absurd to praise such violence, you know?

A: Enfim, está vendo? Os dois estão bem, não foi nada grave.

Look, you see? The two are just fine, it wasn't anything serious.

3
Language focus
Focus 1
Imperfeito x Perfeito — ação interrompida
English: "were doing" (in progress) + "happened" (one event)
"Os dois bateram a cabeça quando iam para a bola" combines two past tenses: the imperfeito (iam — "were going") describes an action already in progress, and the perfeito (bateram — "hit") marks the single completed event that interrupted it. This "was doing X when Y happened" pattern is one of the most useful in everyday Portuguese storytelling.

Imperfeito (em andamento)

eles iam para a bola

eu estava lendo

Perfeito (evento único)

eles bateram a cabeça

o telefone tocou

Os dois bateram a cabeça quando iam para a bola.
The two hit their heads when they were going for the ball. Eu estava lendo quando o telefone tocou.
I was reading when the phone rang.
Focus 2
Como se + imperfeito do subjuntivo — "as if..."
English: as if it were / as if he had / as if she were -ing
Como se ("as if") triggers a special past subjunctive form, but at this level you just need to recognize and use a few very common ready-made combinations — think of them as fixed chunks rather than a full conjugation drill: como se fosse (as if it were), como se tivesse (as if [someone] had), como se estivesse (as if [someone] were -ing).
Vocês assistindo como se fosse diversão.
You watching as if it were entertainment. Ele fala como se soubesse tudo.
He talks as if he knew everything.
Focus 3
Vocabulário do futebol & expressões do diálogo
Soccer vocabulary & everyday conversational expressions
Key vocabulary and conversational markers from this friendly disagreement about soccer's rough side.
Que que houve? — What happened? (colloquial, from "o que que houve")
Viu? — you know? / see? (very common conversational tag)
Não foi nada grave — it wasn't anything serious
Elogiar — to praise
Se identificar com — to identify with
Machucado / lesionado — hurt / injured
⚽ Culture note Futebol is central to Brazilian culture, and passionate debates about the sport — its beauty, its violence, its stars — are a national pastime in themselves. Disagreeing about futebol with a friend, like in this dialogue, is extremely common and rarely personal!
🔊 Phonology note "Que que houve?" doubles up que (from "o que é que houve?") — a very casual, spoken-only contraction. You'll hear this doubling pattern ("que que", "quem que", "cadê que") constantly in informal Brazilian speech.
