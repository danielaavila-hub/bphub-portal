Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Ir + infinitivo — "Going to…"

B says "Eu vou vomitar" — I'm going to throw up. This structure — ir + infinitive — is the most common way to talk about the near future in Brazilian Portuguese. It works exactly like "going to" in English.

Subject Ir Example Meaning
Eu vou Eu vou vomitar. I'm going to throw up.
Você / Ele / Ela vai Ele vai ao médico. He's going to the doctor.
A gente vai A gente vai descansar. We're going to rest.
Vocês / Eles / Elas vão Eles vão sair cedo. They're going to leave early.

To make it negative: não + ir + infinitive. → "Eu não vou comer nada." — I'm not going to eat anything.

📖 Key Expressions from the Dialogue
Portuguese English Notes
Que que é? What's up? / What is it? Very informal contraction of O que é que é? Common in everyday speech — signals concern or curiosity.
Rapaz Man / Buddy / Dude Literally "young man". Used informally to address a male friend. Similar to cara (more common in SP) or mano.
Eu não estou me sentindo bem. I'm not feeling well. Key health expression. Sentir-se = to feel (reflexive). Me sentindo = feeling (gerund form with reflexive pronoun).
Que que houve? What happened? Informal contraction of O que foi que houve?. Houve = past tense of haver (to happen/occur). A very natural follow-up question.
Eu vou vomitar. I'm going to throw up. Ir + infinitivo = going to. Vomitar is the standard word; colloquially Brazilians also say passar mal (to feel sick).
🔊 Phonology Note — "Que que é?" — speech reduction The full form O que é que é? gets compressed into Que que é? in casual speech — four words become three, and the "o" at the start disappears entirely. Similarly, O que foi que houve? → Que que houve?. This kind of rapid reduction is very characteristic of informal Brazilian Portuguese and happens constantly in everyday conversation. You'll hear it, but you may not be ready for it the first time!
🇧🇷 Culture Note — "Rapaz" and informal address Brazilians use a rich set of informal terms to address friends. Rapaz (literally "young man/boy") is common in many regions. In São Paulo you'll often hear cara (literally "face"). In Rio and elsewhere, mano/mana (from irmão/irmã, brother/sister) is popular. All of these function like "man", "dude", or "mate" in English — they signal closeness and informality.

1 What does it mean? — Expression Quiz
Each expression comes from the dialogue. Choose the correct English meaning.
Que que é, rapaz?
✅ Correct! Que que é? is a very casual "What's up?" / "What is it?" — here A is noticing something is wrong with B and checking in.
Não estou me sentindo bem.
✅ Correct! Sentir-se bem = to feel well. This is a fixed and very useful expression — memorise it whole! Notice the present continuous: estou me sentindo.
Que que houve?
✅ Correct! Que que houve? = What happened? Houve is the past tense of haver. A natural follow-up when someone doesn't look well.
Eu vou vomitar.
✅ Correct! Vou + infinitive = going to. Vomitar = to vomit / throw up. B is warning A — urgently!
