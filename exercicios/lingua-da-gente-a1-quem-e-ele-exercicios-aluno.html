Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Aumentativo com -ão — Making things bigger and more intense

A describes the man as "altão, bonitão" — not just tall and handsome, but really tall and really handsome. The suffix -ão (feminine: -ona) adds size, intensity, or admiration. It's one of the most expressive features of everyday Brazilian Portuguese.

Base adjective Augmentative (m.) Augmentative (f.) Meaning
alto (tall) altão altona really tall
bonito (handsome) bonitão bonitona really handsome / gorgeous
grande (big) grandão grandona really big / large
legal (cool / nice) legalão legalona really cool / awesome
gostoso (tasty / attractive) gostosão gostosona really attractive / delicious
💡 -ão vs. muito Both muito alto and altão mean "very tall", but they feel very different. Muito is neutral and descriptive. -ão is expressive, enthusiastic, and typically spoken — it carries a sense of admiration or exaggeration. When A says "aquele altão, bonitão", you can almost hear the swoon in the voice!
📖 Demonstrativos — Este, esse, aquele

B points to the man with "aquele ali" — that one over there. Portuguese has three levels of distance for demonstratives.

Demonstrative Distance Example Meaning
Este / Esta Close to the speaker Este livro aqui. This book (right here).
Esse / Essa Close to the listener Esse cara aí. That guy (near you).
Aquele / Aquela Far from both Aquele ali do lado. That one over there.

In everyday Brazilian speech, esse/essa is often used even for things close to the speaker — the three-way distinction is becoming less strict informally. But aquele is clearly reserved for things at a visible distance.

📖 Key Expressions from the Dialogue
Portuguese English Notes
Um… Um… / Uh… Hesitation filler — universal! Brazilians use um or é… the same way English speakers say "um" or "uh".
Quem é ele? Who is he? Quem = who. Used for people only. Compare: O que é isso? = What is that? (for things).
Aquele ali do lado. That one over on the side. Ali = over there (specific). Do lado = on the side / nearby. Together they point precisely.
Altão, bonitão. Really tall, really handsome. Two augmentatives in a row — emphatic and expressive. A is clearly impressed!
O primo do Carlinhos. Carlinhos' cousin. Possession: de + o = do. Primo = (male) cousin. Note: -inho on Carlos → Carlinhos (affectionate diminutive — very common with Brazilian names).
🇧🇷 Culture Note — "-inho" on names: Carlinhos, Zezinho, Mariazinha Brazilians love to add -inho / -inha to names as a sign of affection. Carlos → Carlinhos. José → Zezinho. Maria → Mariazinha. This doesn't mean the person is young or small — it simply signals closeness and warmth. If someone calls you by a diminutive of your name in Brazil, it's a sign they like you!
🔊 Phonology Note — "Ah ele, ele é…" — repetition for emphasis Notice how B says "Ah ele, ele é o primo do Carlinhos" — repeating ele twice. This kind of repetition is very natural in Brazilian speech when clarifying or confirming who you mean. The first ele is a recognition marker ("oh, him") and the second starts the actual sentence. Don't be surprised when you hear this pattern!

Step 4 — Practice

1 What does it mean? — Expression Quiz
Each expression comes from the dialogue. Choose the correct English meaning.
Aquele ali do lado.
✅ Correct! Aquele = that (far away). Ali = over there. Do lado = on the side. Together they point to someone visible but at a distance.
Altão, bonitão.
✅ Correct! The suffix -ão intensifies — altão = really/very tall, bonitão = really handsome. It's more expressive than just saying muito alto!
O primo do Carlinhos.
✅ Correct! Primo = (male) cousin. Do = de + o (of the / 's). Carlinhos is the affectionate diminutive of Carlos — very typical in Brazilian Portuguese.
Quem é ele?
✅ Correct! Quem = who (used for people). Compare: onde = where, como = how, o que = what.
