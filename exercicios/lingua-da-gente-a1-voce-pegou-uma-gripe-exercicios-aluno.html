Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Pegar — to catch / to get (something)

A says "você pegou uma gripe" — pegar (literally: to grab/take) is used in everyday Brazilian Portuguese in many health and physical contexts. It's a regular -ar verb.

Portuguese English Context
pegar uma gripe to catch a cold Health — most common use
pegar um táxi to catch / take a taxi Transport
pegar sol to sunbathe / to catch sun Very common at the beach
pegar fila to queue up / to wait in line Everyday life
pegar trânsito to get stuck in traffic Very common in Brazilian cities
🔊 "Pegou sim" — affirmative reinforcement When B denies having a cold, A insists: "Não, você pegou sim." Adding sim after the verb is a powerful Brazilian way to affirm something the other person has just denied — like English "Yes you did!" or "You really did." You'll hear this constantly in everyday disagreements: Fui sim! (I did go!), Eu disse sim! (I did say it!). It turns a simple statement into a firm, emphatic one.
📖 Já tomou? — Já + preterite for recent actions

A asks "Você já tomou alguma coisa?" — Here já + preterite asks whether something has already happened. You've seen já in several episodes — this is its "already" meaning applied as a question about the recent past.

Question with já English Answer
Você já tomou o remédio? Have you taken the medicine yet / already? Já tomei. / Ainda não.
Você já comeu? Have you eaten yet? Já comi. / Ainda não.
Você já foi ao médico? Have you been to the doctor yet? Já fui. / Ainda não fui.

Tomar in health contexts means "to take" (medicine, vitamins, a pill). Key health phrases with tomar: tomar um remédio (to take a medicine), tomar vitamina C (to take vitamin C), tomar um chá (to have a herbal tea). Alguma coisa = anything / something.

📖 O remédio que… — Relative pronoun + object pronouns

B says "o remédio que a Marina me deu." Two important patterns in one sentence: the relative pronoun que and the indirect object pronoun me.

Pattern Example Notes
que (relative pronoun) o remédio que ela me deu (the medicine that she gave me) Links the noun to a relative clause. Que works for people and things — no distinction like English who/that/which
me (indirect object) A Marina me deu o remédio. (Marina gave me the medicine.) Me = to me. Comes before the verb in Brazilian Portuguese spoken style
muita vitamina C tomei muita vitamina C (I took a lot of vitamin C) Muito/muita agrees with the noun: muito suco (m.) / muita água (f.)
🇧🇷 Remédios caseiros — Home remedies in Brazil Brazilians rely heavily on remédios caseiros (home remedies) alongside conventional medicine. Vitamin C is a go-to, but you'll also hear: chá de gengibre com mel (ginger tea with honey), chá de erva-cidreira (lemon balm tea), sopa de galinha (chicken soup — yes, universal!), and mel puro (pure honey) for sore throats. Pharmacies (farmácias) in Brazil are everywhere and you can get many medications without a prescription that require one elsewhere. "Já tomou alguma coisa?" is a very natural Brazilian way to check in on a sick friend or family member.

Step 4 — Practice

1 What does it mean? — Expression Quiz
Choose the correct meaning for each expression from the dialogue.
Você pegou uma gripe.
✅ Correct! Pegou = preterite of pegar (you caught / you've caught). Uma gripe = a cold / flu. Pegar uma gripe is the standard Brazilian expression for "to catch a cold."
Você pegou sim.
✅ Correct! Sim after the verb is emphatic affirmation — used when the other person has denied something. Pegou sim = You really did catch it / Yes you did catch it.
Você já tomou alguma coisa?
✅ Correct! Já + preterite = have you already / yet. Tomou = preterite of tomar (to take). Alguma coisa = anything / something. Together: Have you taken anything (yet)?
O remédio que a Marina me deu.
✅ Correct! Que = that (relative pronoun). Me deu = gave me (me = indirect object "to me"; deu = preterite of dar). So: the medicine that Marina gave (to) me.
