Step 3 — Language Focus

📖 Vamos + infinitivo — "Let's…"

Vamos + infinitive is the simplest and most natural way to suggest doing something together in Portuguese — just like "let's" in English. No conjugation to worry about!

Portuguese English
Vamos cantar! Let's sing!
Vamos comer! Let's eat!
Vamos sair! Let's go out!
Vamos falar português! Let's speak Portuguese!
Vamos embora! Let's go! (time to leave)
💡 "Gente" as a vocative In "Vamos cantar, gente!", the word gente is used as a vocative — addressing the group directly, like "guys" or "everyone" in English. This is incredibly common in Brazilian speech: "Gente, que saudade!" / "Gente, olha que lindo!". It adds warmth and informality to any invitation.
📖 Saber + infinitivo — "to know how to…"

B says "Eu não sei cantar" — I don't know how to sing. In Portuguese, use saber + infinitive to talk about skills and abilities.

Subject Saber Example Meaning
Eu sei Eu sei cantar. I know how to sing.
Você / Ele / Ela sabe Você sabe cozinhar? Do you know how to cook?
A gente sabe A gente sabe dançar. We know how to dance.
Vocês / Eles / Elas sabem Eles sabem jogar futebol. They know how to play football.

To say you don't know how: Eu não sei + infinitive. → "Eu não sei cantar." / "Eu não sei cozinhar."

📖 Key Expressions from the Dialogue
Portuguese English Notes
Vamos cantar, gente! Let's sing, guys! Enthusiastic invitation. Gente as vocative makes it warm and inclusive.
Canta você. You sing. / You go ahead and sing. Redirecting the suggestion back. The subject você comes after the verb for emphasis.
Eu não sei cantar. I don't know how to sing. Classic excuse! Saber + infinitive = to know how to do something.
E daí? So what? / So? Dismissive but friendly. Used to brush off an excuse or objection. Tone matters — it can be teasing or impatient.
Eu também não canto bem. I don't sing well either. Também não = not… either / me neither. Very useful for agreeing with a negative statement.
Mentira! Liar! / No way! / That's not true! Literally "lie/falsehood". Used informally to contradict someone in a playful way — not aggressive!
Quase profissional. Almost professionally. Quase = almost. Notice the informal structure — no adverb suffix needed in Portuguese here.
🇧🇷 Culture Note — "Mentira!" as a compliment In Brazil, Mentira! in this context is not an insult — it's a playful contradiction. B uses it to push back on A's false modesty: "You almost sing professionally!" This kind of teasing back-and-forth, with exaggerated denials and compliments, is very typical of informal Brazilian conversation. It's a sign of closeness and affection.
🔊 Phonology Note — "E daí" — tone and attitude E daí? is two short words, but the way they're said carries everything. Typically pronounced quickly with a slight rising tone — "e-daí?" — it sounds breezy and encouraging. Said with a flat tone it can sound impatient. Listen to how A uses it in the audio: it's warm, not confrontational — the message is "don't worry, let's just have fun!"
