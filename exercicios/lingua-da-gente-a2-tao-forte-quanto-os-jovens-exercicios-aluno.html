Tão Forte Quanto os Jovens

A doctor checks a patient's blood pressure, and the good news reveals healthy habit changes. Talking about giving something up with parar de, and comparing equally with tão... quanto.

Read the dialogue

At a check-up, a doctor takes a patient's blood pressure and hears about his healthier new habits.

A: Vamos tirar a pressão também.

Let's take your blood pressure too.

B: Acho que vai ser melhor, parei de comer tanto sal.

I think it's going to be better, I have stopped eating so much salt.

A: Vamos ver, 12 por 8, muito bom, melhorou bastante.

Let's see, 120 over 80, very good, it has improved a lot.

B: Tá vendo doutor, tão forte quanto os jovens!

You see doctor, as strong as the young ones!

A: Ah é? E a cafeína, você ainda está tomando o café?

Oh yes? And the caffeine, are you still drinking coffee?

B: Muito menos, não tomo nada depois das 18:00hrs.

Much less, I don't drink any after 6:00pm.

3
Language focus
Focus 1
Parar de + infinitivo — pretérito perfeito
English: I stopped / gave up doing something
Parar de + infinitive means "to stop / give up" doing something. Parei is the pretérito perfeito (simple past) form, used to describe a habit change that already happened.
Parei de comer tanto sal.
I stopped eating so much salt. Ela parou de fumar há um ano.
She quit smoking a year ago.
Focus 2
Tão... quanto — comparação de igualdade
English: As... as (equal comparison)
Tão + adjective + quanto compares two things as equal — the Portuguese equivalent of English "as... as."
Tão forte quanto os jovens!
As strong as the young ones! Este café é tão bom quanto aquele.
This coffee is as good as that one.
Focus 3
Ainda + presente & vocabulário de saúde
English: still + present tense (asking if a habit continues) + health vocabulary
Ainda + present tense asks whether something is still happening — a natural way doctors (and friends) check in on ongoing habits.
Você ainda está tomando café?
Are you still drinking coffee? Pressão — blood pressure · Sal — salt
Cafeína — caffeine · Melhorar — to improve
🩺 Culture note Blood pressure in Brazil is typically read as "doze por oito" (12 by 8), shorthand for 120/80 mmHg — Brazilians drop the last zero when speaking, so always double it mentally when you hear numbers like this at the doctor's office.
🔊 Phonology note Notice the stress pattern in tão — a nasal diphthong that can trip up English speakers. Practice it alongside quanto, which also carries a nasal "an" sound distinct from English.
