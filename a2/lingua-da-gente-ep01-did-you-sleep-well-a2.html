Diálogo

Did You Sleep Well?

Listen first, then read along. Pay attention to how A and B take turns.

A
Bom dia, você dormiu bem?
Good morning, did you sleep well?
B
Mais ou menos. Que horas são?
More or less. What time is it?
A
Seis e meia, ainda é muito cedo.
Six-thirty, it's still really early.
B
Vamos dormir um pouco mais!
Let's sleep a little more!
A
Tá, mais um pouquinho só.
OK, just a tiny bit more.
Vocabulário em contexto

Key Words & Expressions

These words come directly from the dialogue. Study them in context.

bom dia
good morning
— Bom dia! Como você está?
dormiu bem?
did you sleep well?
From dormir (to sleep). Você dormiu bem? — Did you sleep well?
mais ou menos
more or less / so-so
Very common in Brazil! — Como você está? Mais ou menos.
que horas são?
what time is it?
Que horas são? — É meia-noite.
seis e meia
six-thirty (6:30)
Meia = half. So seis e meia = six and a half = 6:30.
ainda
still / yet
Ainda é cedo. — It's still early.
cedo
early
Opposite: tarde (late). Cheguei cedo. — I arrived early.
um pouco / pouquinho
a little / a tiny bit
Pouquinho is the diminutive — even smaller than pouco. Very Brazilian!
tá
OK / alright
Informal contraction of está bem. Super common in spoken Brazilian Portuguese.
Gramática

Telling Time in Portuguese

In the dialogue, B asks Que horas são? and A replies Seis e meia. Here's the full pattern:

The question

Que horas são? What time is it?

Literally: "What hours are they?" — always use the plural são for any time except 1:00.

São duas horas. It's two o'clock. (plural)
É uma hora. It's one o'clock. (singular)

Gramática

Making Suggestions: Vamos + infinitive

In the dialogue, B says Vamos dormir um pouco mais! This structure is the most natural way to make a "let's…" suggestion in Brazilian Portuguese.

Pattern

Vamos + infinitive verb

Vamos dormir um pouco mais! Let's sleep a little more!
Vamos comer? Shall we eat? / Let's eat!
Vamos trabalhar! Let's get to work!
Vamos começar? Shall we start?

💡 Vamos with a question mark sounds like an invitation; with an exclamation mark, it's more enthusiastic. Both are very common.

Cultura

Language & Culture Notes

🇧🇷 Brazilian Portuguese in real life

"Mais ou menos" is much more common in Brazil than a simple "sim" or "não" when answering how you are. Brazilians tend to avoid extreme answers in casual conversation — mais ou menos is a friendly, honest, relaxed response.


"Tá" (from está bem) is everywhere in informal Brazilian speech. You'll hear it constantly as a filler, agreement marker, or conversation connector. Learning it will make your Portuguese sound much more natural immediately.


Diminutives are a hallmark of Brazilian warmth. Pouquinho (from pouco) doesn't just mean "less" — it makes the request softer, more affectionate. Brazilians use diminutives frequently to add warmth, urgency, or humor to everyday speech.

Pronúncia
🔊 Phonology tip for English speakers

Notice the nasal vowels in this dialogue: bom, mais, ainda. In Brazilian Portuguese, vowels before m or n are nasalized — the sound resonates through your nose. This is very different from English. Practice: say bom and feel the air flow through your nose at the end. Compare bom dia (nasal "bom") with "bone" in English (not nasal). Listen to the dialog audio several times focusing on these sounds.
